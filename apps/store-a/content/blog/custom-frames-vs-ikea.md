IKEA and other big-box frames are a **perfectly rational** choice for mass-market poster sizes, tight timelines, and replaceable prints. Custom framing enters when **exact geometry**, **conservation**, or **dimensional objects** make readymades fight the art instead of supporting it.

![Standard readymade frame interior showing fixed mat opening versus trimmable custom window concept](/images/blog/frame-studio-hero.png)

## Where readymades win

- **Speed:** grab, clip, hang—same day.
- **Price:** economies of scale on common sizes (8×10, 12×16, 18×24, 24×36).
- **Predictability:** what you see on shelf is (usually) what fits—_if_ your print matches.

## Where readymades quietly lose

### 1. The paper is not quite “standard”

Labs trim differently. Digital prints creep by fractional inches. Vintage sheets are imperial-metric soup. A **1/16″** mismatch can mean visible gaps, crooked window reveals, or forced cropping.

### 2. Glazing and backing are built to a price point

Budget glazing may lack the **UV** or **anti-reflective** qualities you want for long-display keepsakes. That is not a moral judgment—just a **use-case** judgment.

### 3. Depth is fixed

Anything with modest thickness—**signed baseballs, textiles, pins, medals, flowers,** thick impasto—needs **rabbet depth** and spacers readymades rarely provide.

![Shadowbox depth diagram concept with spacers between art object and glazing](/images/blog/vinyl-frame-hero.png)

### 4. Mat design is one-size-fits-many

You inherit somebody else’s border ratios and colors. Gallery-style **weighted bottoms**, **double-mat reveals**, and **multi-window** layouts are custom territory.

## Custom: pay for elimination of compromise

Custom isn’t only “fancier wood.” It is **the opening you want**, the **stack depth you need**, and the **glazing tier you choose**—synchronized so the back closes cleanly and ships safely.

### When custom is the smart financial move

If a readymade forces you to **buy twice** (frame + reprint + time), custom was cheaper all along. Measure once, echoing the mantra from our measuring guide.

## Hybrid strategies that work

- Use readymades for **temporary** dorm décor; invest custom for **archival** pieces.
- Buy readymades for **true** standard poster sizes; go custom for **A-series** sheets and odd trims.
- Pair inexpensive metal readymades with **professional mats** only when depth matches—otherwise the glass can kiss the surface.

## The upgrade path (without throwing away old frames)

Sometimes you keep a readymade **outer** but replace interior glazing with conservation acrylic, add a better mount, or stack a new custom mat inside—**only** when rabbet math cooperates. If the shortcut feels forced, graduate to full custom before missing a deadline.

## Sustainability reality check

Readymades consumed quickly create **short-cycle waste**. Custom sized correctly tends to **persist** on walls—an environmental angle people rarely discuss alongside dollars.

## When brand loyalty is backwards

Love IKEA for what it does well—storage, desks, lamps—and love custom for **geometry** problems readymades pretend do not exist. Mixing philosophies room-by-room is normal in real homes.

## Ribba, Lomviken, and the psychology of SKU names

Customers often remember IKEA lines by color and chamfer, not model numbers—that is fine until you need **replacement parts** two years later. Readymade ecosystems assume continuity; when a line retires, you **chase auctions** or pivot styles. Custom archives your **spec** in order history instead of hoping Stockholm still stocks the clip.

## Education markets: teachers, coaches, labs

Schools burn through **poster refreshes** yearly; IKEA wins on price. Hall-of-fame jerseys and permanent installation plaques eventually graduate to **custom** once the object’s lifespan outlasts the budget cycle.

## When resale value matters

Renters sometimes think cheap frames “protect security deposits.” True—but **patch strategies** matter more than moulding cost. A heavy, poorly anchored frame damages more drywall than a lighter package hung correctly.

## Instruction manuals vs craft knowledge

IKEA instructions excel at **repeatable assembly**; framing excels at **conditional rules**—when to float, when to lift mats, when to refuse a shallow rabbet. If your gut says “this fits but feels wrong,” trust the physics whispers.

## Seasonal decor vs archival intent

Holiday rotate-ins love readymades because turnover is the point. Milestone artifacts—wedding contracts, first ultrasound printouts, immigration certificates—deserve **conditional** thinking, not seasonal pricing reflexes.

## Accessibility and opening pressure

Elder relatives wrestling **springy steel clips** on readymades sometimes re-injure wrists—custom packages with sensible turn buttons or professional fitting can be an accessibility win disguised as luxury.

## Long-distance relationships to objects

If you **ship heirlooms** cross-country to custodial siblings, readymade glass in under-packed cartons is grief accelerant. Custom shops spec packaging with **object trauma** in mind; thrift frames assume you own bubble wrap futures.

## Crowdfunding fulfillment realities

Kickstarted art prints often ship **non-standard** sizes because artists chase faithful aspect ratios—backers expecting Ribba salvation learn geometry lessons publicly. Communicate **custom budgeting** in campaign FAQs early.

## Emotional discounting

“We’ll just grab something cheap for now” sometimes survives a decade—cheap becomes **de facto permanent**. If the object whispers permanence, listen before checkout.

## Warranty literacy

Read **what “lifetime” modifies**—hardware lifetime, joint integrity, glamour photos? Custom purchases deserve the same skepticism you bring to electronics warranties.

## Landlord beige vs renter personality

Renters fear **investment** in custom because “I might move.” Portable collections love **standard-ish outer sizes** that survive apartment hopping; lifelong trophies ignore landlords entirely.

## Pop-up markets and fair booths

Vendors often sell prints sized for **speed**, not for Ribba truth—carry a tape measure at fairs if you sell companion framing packages or risk buyer regret DMs.

## When hype cycles expire

Trendy neon mouldings age faster than **honest walnut**. Readymades chase trend churn; custom can deliberately chase **long half-life** if you say so out loud during design.
