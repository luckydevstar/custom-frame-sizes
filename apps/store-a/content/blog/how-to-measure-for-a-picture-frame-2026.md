If you have ever received a frame that was “almost” right, the culprit is usually **what got measured**, not carpentry. In 2026, most shoppers combine physical prints, digital downloads with odd aspect ratios, and mixed borders. This workflow keeps you out of trouble whether you are framing an 8×10 snapshot or a 17.25″ fine-art sheet.

![Mat window overlapping a photograph edge to illustrate typical mat overlap allowance](/images/blog/botanical-frame-hero.png)

## Start with the goal: what must be visible?

Before you touch a ruler, answer three questions:

1. **Do you want the outer paper edge visible** (full sheet) or only the image area?
2. **Will a mat cover the edges** of the print? If yes, decide the **reveal** (how much of the image shows inside the mat window).
3. **Is anything dimensional**—deckled edges, impasto paint, or lift from a mount? That affects depth, not just width and height.

Write these answers down. They become constraints when you translate numbers into a designer.

## Tools that actually help

- A **metal ruler** or reliable tape (avoid cloth sewing tapes for final dimensions).
- Good lighting so you can see whether the print is square.
- For thin papers, a **flat weight** in the corners so the sheet does not curl while measuring.
- Optional: **digital calipers** for tiny editions or thick stacks.

## Step 1: Measure the art at multiple points

For rectangular prints:

- Measure **width** near the top and bottom; use the **smaller** value if they differ.
- Measure **height** near the left and right; again use the **smaller** value if they differ.

If the difference is more than **1/16″**, your art may be skewed or trimmed unevenly—note it in your order comments and consider a slightly larger opening or a wider mat to hide irregular edges.

### Digital files

If you are printing at a lab, confirm **final trimmed size** after bleed. Many “18×24″ posters” finish closer to 17.875×23.875 depending on the printer. Always measure **the item you will frame**, not the file’s nominal size.

## Step 2: Choose your “opening” size

The **opening** is what the frame (or mat window) needs to clear.

### Full-bleed looks

If the image runs to the edge and you want zero border visible, the opening typically matches the visible image size with a tiny manufacturing allowance—your framer or designer documentation should specify that allowance (often a small fraction of an inch).

### Prints with a white border

Decide whether the border is part of the look. Some collectors mat **over** the border; others **float** the entire sheet so the deckle shows. Each choice changes the opening and the mat or spacer package.

## Step 3: Account for mat overlap

When a mat rests on top of the art, the window is **slightly smaller** than the image so the art does not fall through. A common starting range is **1/8″** total overlap on each dimension (discuss specifics with your vendor). That means:

- Opening width ≈ visible image width − overlap total
- Opening height ≈ visible image height − overlap total

If you forget overlap, the mat window can look “gappy” or expose uneven edges.

## Step 4: Translate to outer frame size

The **outer** frame dimensions include moulding width. You usually do not calculate moulding manually in a designer—you pick a profile and the tool computes outside dimensions. What _you_ supply is the **package size**: rabbet width/height that accepts your stack (art + backing + any matboard layers).

Beginners should focus on **inside/rabbet** requirements and let the configurator handle outside width unless you have a tight niche (for example, fitting inside a historic built-in).

## Common 2026 mistakes we still see

- Measuring the **outside** of an existing frame instead of the **art**.
- Using “closest standard size” when the art is **non-standard**—common with European A-series prints mixed into US homes.
- Ignoring **signature lines** under the image that need air in the mat window.
- Forgetting that **dry-mounted** pieces cannot always be safely removed—note mounting before ordering.

## Accessibility and shipping checks

If the finished piece will ship, verify wall weight limits and whether **acrylic** suits your use case. Heavy glass in large sizes may require upgraded hardware or professional installation.

When your numbers are stable, move into a tool that validates depth and glazing at the same time—you should not finalize glass before you finalize mat thickness and object depth.

**Next step:** open the **[custom frame designer](/designer)** and enter your opening dimensions exactly as documented above. If you already know you need depth for objects, compare regular versus shadowbox-style configurations before you lock materials.

## Odd shapes, decks, and stubborn warps

**Ovals and circles** still get bounding-box measurements, but note the **true diameter** and whether the framer will cut a circular window or square opening—those are different conversations.

**Deckled edges** reward **float mounts** where the sheet hovers above a backing. Measure the **full sheet** including deckle, then decide visible margin in the designer rather than cropping blindly.

**Wavy paper** can be coaxed flat with controlled humidity and weight—or faced with corner mounts. Document whether waviness is **temporary curl** or **permanent cockling**; the mount strategy changes.

## Room-to-room measurement etiquette

Measure where the **frame will live**, not where you unwrapped it. Humidity differences between basement workbenches and upstairs halls subtly change paper flatness—usually minor, but worth noting for oversized pieces.

## Export a “measurement card” for your order notes

Professionals love a tiny sketch: width × height, arrow to “top,” circle any fragile zones, and list signatures. Paste that into order comments when you buy online—it prevents assumptions.

## Working with framer’s assistants remotely

Video calls can verify **tape pulls** on corners if you are unsure the sheet is square—have a friend film slowly while you hold the ruler still. Pixelated Zoom arithmetic is still better than no second eyes.

## Legal and civic documents

Some certificates include **seals** that cannot flex. Note **raised emblem thickness** explicitly—this is where flat-frame assumptions die heroically.

Snap a cellphone photo with a ruler in-frame for every certificate—pixels are free, re-orders are not.
