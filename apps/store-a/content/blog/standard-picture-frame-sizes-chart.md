Standard sizes exist because cameras, labs, and big-box inventory all love repeatable numbers. The chart below is a **practical US-oriented reference** for the sizes you will see in stores and online—plus guidance on when “close enough” hurts the look and a custom opening is smarter.

![Row of frame moulding samples beside a poster to show standard poster framing proportions](/images/blog/frame-studio-hero.png)

## Common photo sizes (inches)

These sizes dominate desks, hallways, and gift prints:

| Size (W×H) | Typical source        | Framing notes                               |
| ---------- | --------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| 4×6        | Prints, postcards     | Often matted to 8×10 for presence           |
| 5×7        | Portraits             | Popular for desk frames; mat upgrade common |
| 8×10       | Portrait sessions     | Matting to 11×14 or 16×20 reads “gallery”   |
| 11×14      | Art prints, portraits | Verify actual trim—labs vary slightly       |
| 16×20      | Portraits, medium art | Heavy; plan hardware for glass weight       |

### ISO / “A” sizes

Studios and European suppliers often ship **A4**, **A3**, or **A2**. These do **not** map cleanly to US readymades. If you are tempted to “float” an A3 in a 12×16 frame, you will usually crop or invent filler strips—better to **order the opening to the sheet**.

## Standard poster sizes (inches)

Posters cluster around these widths:

| Size (W×H) | Typical use              | Watch-outs                                     |
| ---------- | ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------------- |
| 12×18      | Promotional, fan art     | Often thin paper—handle corners gently         |
| 18×24      | Concert / museum posters | Check exact trim; many finish slightly smaller |
| 20×30      | Retail décor             | Verify aspect ratio vs your wall niche         |
| 24×36      | Large statement pieces   | Consider acrylic for shipping                  |
| 27×40      | One-sheet movie posters  | Very specific niche; depth for tubes damage    |

## When standard frames fail (and custom wins)

You should move to **custom** when:

- The print is **fractionally** off a standard (common with in-house cutters).
- You want a **specific mat border** mathematically tied to the image.
- The piece includes **signatures**, edition lines, or deckled edges that need breathing room.
- You are building a **gallery wall** with consistent mat borders across mismatched art sizes.
- You need **depth** for anything beyond a thin paper stack.

Custom does not always mean “expensive art.” It means **no compromises** on what stays visible.

## Mat multiples and visual balance

Designers often “step up” photo sizes in predictable jumps—8×10 in 11×14, 11×14 in 16×20—because even mat borders are easy to scan. For posters, decide whether you want **full-bleed** (no mat) or a **generous border** that makes the image feel intentional rather than “retail poster in a clip frame.”

## Digital aspect ratios vs real paper

Phone panoramas, cropped selfies, and illustration exports can land on sizes that **no** readymade respects. Before you buy a frame on impulse, measure the actual sheet or the exact printable area you intend to trim.

## Using this chart with the designer

Treat standard sizes as **starting points**. In our **[custom frame designer](/designer)**, enter the **true opening** you need—not the nominal store label. If you are between moulding weights, choose profiles after you know your glazing choice (glass vs acrylic) and shipping path.

## Metric neighbors (quick mental map)

US framing dominates North American homes, but **A4 (210×297 mm)** and **A3 (297×420 mm)** often sneak in via online poster shops. There is no magical US readymade that honors those exactly—plan a **custom opening** and stop fighting “almost” fits.

If you already live in a metric-first country, invert the lesson: **US vintage sheets** can look “wrong” in ISO frames. Measure the object on the table, then translate to your designer in **the units it accepts**—consistency beats pride.

## DIY mats on standard sizes (hybrid workflow)

Some customers buy readymade **outer metal frames** but drop in custom **window mats** cut to fractional openings. That only works when **depth** still clears your stack—if the readymade’s glazing kisses the mat because the supplier cheaped out on rabbet depth, hybrid plans fail. Depth is not a suggestion.

## Collector editions and “weird” decimals

Photogravures, modern giclées, and signed runs sometimes ship at **oddball decimals** to preserve aspect ratio integrity. That is a feature, not a mistake—treat the chart as **orientation**, not **constraint**.

## Wall niche math: when the architecture dictates the frame

Bookshelves, paneling offsets, and HVAC soffits often leave **non-negotiable rectangles** on the wall. Start from the **maximum outer frame size** the niche tolerates, then **subtract moulding width** mentally to see how much room remains for mats. Designers sometimes pursue tall mats and skinny mouldings specifically to **win back image area** inside a tight height cap.

If your niche is only an inch wider than a standard poster size, you might still need **custom** to recover balanced mat borders—otherwise the frame feels wedged like a DVD in a shoebox.

## Print services: ask these three questions

1. **“What is the trimmed size after bleed?”**
2. **“Do you center crop or fit long edge?”**
3. **“Is the paper fiber direction consistent batch-to-batch?”** (curl behaves differently in humid weeks)

Answers belong in your order notes upstream of framing so nobody “fixes” a problem that should have been solved at the printer.

## Shipping tubes vs flat packs

Tube-shipped posters often **spring curl**—plan flattening time under supervised weight before final measures. Flat packs cost more at checkout but save frustration if your deadline is real.

## Yearly refresh cadence

Families reprinting annual school photos can **standardize** mat outer sizes while letting inner windows chase growth spurts—future swaps swap windows only if you plan rabbet and stack depth kindly from year one.
