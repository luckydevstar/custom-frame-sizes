The names sound interchangeable at the home store, but **picture frames** and **shadowboxes** solve different physics problems. If your mistake is picking the wrong category, no amount of pretty moulding fixes the **collision** between your object and the glazing.

![Flat framed photo beside a deep frame displaying small dimensional keepsakes on a riser](/images/blog/botanical-frame-hero.png)

## Picture frame (flat package)

**Built for:** Posters, photographs, thin prints, flat certificates, most works on paper.

**Stack typically includes:** art, optional mat(s), mounting board, backing, modest depth, glazing.

**Fails when:** Anything thicker than the allowed stack presses against the glass—jerseys, bats, flowers, medals, chunky canvas boards without adequate float space, or layered scrapbooks.

## Shadowbox (deep package)

**Built for:** Dimensional objects, textiles, collections, artifacts that need **air** between surface and glazing.

**Stack adds:** deeper rabbet, often spacers or liners, creative pinning/sewing mounts, sometimes fabric-wrapped interiors.

**Fails when:** You **overstuff** depth—bulging backs stress corners—or underspec glazing for shipping weight.

## The decision checklist

Answer yes/no:

1. **Does any part of the art rise above the mount plane more than ~1/8–1/4″?** If yes, verify depth; likely shadowbox territory.
2. **Will the item shift if jostled?** Objects need pin mounts, clear tabs, stitched supports, or risers—not just a slip of tape.
3. **Is UV or anti-glare critical?** Dimensional displays still live in bright rooms—**glazing tiers** matter as much here as for flat prints.
4. **Will this ship?** Acrylic and robust corner protection become more important as depth and size grow.

## Visual design differences

Shadowboxes often benefit from **relaxed mat borders**—you are curating an object, not just centering a rectangle. Consider **contrasting liners** to separate object color from background fabric and **rhythm** when grouping multiple items (consistent baselines, not scattered float).

## When people pick the wrong type

- **Jerseys** squeezed into flat frames—crease damage and fabric strain.
- **Pressed flowers** touching acrylic—moisture and sticking risks.
- **Medals** torquing wire mounts—glass flex and rattle.
- **Kids’ LEGO builds** wedged—stress cracks in ABS and scratched glazing.

If you flinched reading any of those, take depth seriously.

## Spacers, liners, and why they matter

**Spacers** keep glazing off dimensional surfaces even when a mat is not doing that job. **Liners** (fabric or painted) hide interior wood edges so viewers focus on objects, not construction. Skipping them can look “DIY okay” in photos but **unfinished** in person.

## Dust, bugs, and seal philosophy

Shadowboxes tempt people to “seal forever.” Reality: **diligent backs** and **quality tapes** balance breathability with protection—absolute hermetic DIY seals can trap moisture. Follow supplier guidance rather than improvising foam caulk.

## Teaching displays (schools, nonprofits)

Public installations need **anti-theft**, **shatter strategies**, and **labels** that do not off-gas on textiles. Shadowboxes shine here, but plan maintenance windows—dust sneaks into deep cavities over years.

## Wedding bouquets, baby shoes, and “maybe later” heirlooms

Dimensional keepsakes often sit in closets because people fear **wrong first frames**. If you are unsure between flat and deep, photograph the object on graph paper to estimate footprint, then simulate **two depth budgets**—shallow-safe vs comfortable. Guessing depth is how petals and laces get crushed.

## Acrylic + textile caution

Static cling on some acrylic sheets attracts **loose fibers** from jerseys and gowns. Shops mitigate with techniques you may not replicate at home—another reason to consult pros when objects are irreplaceable.

## Lighting shadowboxes without cooking the contents

LED strips inside frames tempt makers; heat and trapped UV can still stress dyes. If you self-light, favor **low heat**, indirect bounce, and timers—**museum rules** apply more than Instagram suggests.

## Coins, medals, and pinbacks

Thin, heavy, metallic items vibrate under truck miles if pegs are loose. Shadowboxes for transit should assume **abuse**—silent foam, micro-cradles, and restraint without abrasion. Picture frames seldom tolerate that vocabulary.

## Layering storytelling objects

Stacking a ticket stub, program, and photo in one case reads like **narrative collage**—allocate **zones** with stitching or hidden posts so layers do not slump into each other after a season of wall vibration.

## Sports equipment beyond jerseys

Miniature bats, mitts, and cleats introduce **asymmetric weight**—plan hanging hardware for **center of mass**, not geometric center. A tilted shadowbox on day thirty is a lesson learned expensively.

## Conservation humility

If an object lived through wars, migrations, or fires, admit what you do not know—**consult** specialists before baking adhesives into family history. Shadowboxes celebrate stories; they should not become coffins for reversible textiles.

## Scaling mockups cheaply

Before committing rare objects, build **foam-core simulators** at depth—stack foam scraps until your glazing gap feels honest. Five dollars of foam beats five hundred dollars of regret.

## Audio/visual gear adjacent walls

Shadowboxes near bass-heavy speakers may vibrate—consider **isolation pads** behind hangers or relocating sympathetically. Picture frames vibrate too, but **depth stacks** amplify buzz visually if objects tap glass microscopically.

## Pop culture density walls

Movie prop collectors sometimes **tile** many thin shadowboxes—standardize **outer height** so rows align; nothing exposes amateur hour like staggered top lines pretending to be “organic.”

## Disaster preparedness

If you live in shake-prone zones, **museum putty** on bases and conscientious cleats matter more than profile pretty—shadowboxes are top-heavy physics problems pretending to be décor.

## Loaned objects

Borrowed items for exhibitions arrive with **loan agreements** forbidding adhesives—plan mechanical mounts only and photograph compliance like someone’s lawyer cares. Because they might.
