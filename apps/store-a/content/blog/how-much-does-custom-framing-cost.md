Talking about framing prices without specs is like asking how much a car costs—**size**, **materials**, and **complexity** swing the number more than brand alone. This guide breaks down what typically moves a quote so you can plan a project without surprises.

![Price tags sketch concept beside stacked matboard and moulding samples on a workbench](/images/blog/botanical-frame-hero.png)

## The five levers that matter most

1. **Physical size** – Larger pieces use more of everything: moulding length, mat board, glazing area, and shipping volumetric charges.
2. **Glazing tier** – Standard clear vs UV vs anti-reflective “museum-style” coatings are ascending cost steps with real conservation benefits.
3. **Mat complexity** – Single window vs multiple windows, double mats, special cuts, and oversize mat blanks.
4. **Moulding profile and finish** – Exotic woods, hand finishes, wide profiles, and specialty metals carry premium meter costs versus simple painted flats.
5. **Depth and specialty construction** – Shadowbox-style depths, spacers, fabric wraps, and object mounting require labor and hardware beyond flat prints.

## Retail vs online custom: what you are paying for

**Brick-and-mortar shops** bundle design time, local labor, and often rush turnaround—you are buying expertise and touch labor in person.

**Online custom** often prices from **transparent SKUs**: you pick each layer, see upgrades line-by-line, and pay for shipping consolidation rather than metro rent. The trade-off: you must supply **accurate measurements** and good photos if something is unusual.

Neither approach is “always cheaper”—compare **apples-to-apples materials** when shopping quotes.

Small photo-sized packages with economical moulding and standard glazing usually land in the ** approachable** part of the spectrum, while large posters with museum glazing and wide mats land **multiple times higher**—sometimes an order of magnitude on huge walls.

## Hidden costs to avoid

- **Reorders from bad math** – The most expensive frame is the wrong frame.
- **Hardware upgrades** for heavy glass on plaster walls.
- **Rush production** during holidays—plan galleries early.
- **Shipping insurance** awareness for one-of-a-kind art.

## Value engineering without trashing the look

- Use **acrylic** when weight or shipping dominates.
- Pick **UV acrylic or mid-tier UV glass** before skipping UV entirely on keepers.
- Choose **quieter moulding** and let mats carry sophistication—profile price isn’t the only path to a luxe look.
- Standardize **repeat sizes** in a wall series to batch decisions.

## Timing: sales, holidays, and lead times

Framing is sometimes seasonal—Mother’s Day, graduations, December gifting. If you need pieces for an event, pad **production + shipping + buffer** for weather delays. Rush fees exist because labor genuinely reallocates.

## Insurance and declared value

For irreplaceable art, understand **carrier insurance limits** and photograph packages before sealing. The goal is not pessimism—it is **paper trail literacy** if a corner takes a hit.

## When “cheap online” is a trap

If a site will not let you specify **mat overlap**, **glazing tier**, or **rabbet depth**, you are buying a mystery box. Mystery boxes occasionally work for posters; they routinely fail for keepsakes.

## Bulk orders, offices, and matching suites

Ten matching certificate frames for a new HQ sounds uniform—until **doorways** and **elevators** limit **outer dimensions**. Model **one worst-case wall** before you clone SKUs across a floor plate; bulk savings vanish when half the batch needs reconfiguration.

## Trade comparisons done honestly

When someone cites a “$ quote,” ask **which glazing** and **which moulding meter** they modeled—without those, you are comparing folklore. Line items exist to kill folklore.

## Financing psychology

People happily finance **furniture** but hesitate on **protection** for paper heirlooms—invert that instinct when the art’s replacement cost is emotional, not monetary.

## Gift economics

Framing as a gift should budget **design time**—not just dollars—because recipients rarely have your measurements handy. Gift certificates paired with our **[designer](/designer)** reduce awkward guesswork while preserving sentiment.

## Revision loops

Some shops include **one revision pass** in consultation fees; online flows reduce rounds by locking math early. If you oscillate between two mat widths for days, screenshot both previews—humans decide faster with side-by-side ghosts.

## International duties and brokerage

Cross-border shipments occasionally spike totals with **duties** unpredictable at configurator time—model “landed cost” pessimism if deadlines are immovable.

## Currency and exchange students

Families framing abroad for stateside walls should confirm **unit systems** twice—millimeters sneaking into imperial orders cause legendary Thanksgiving arguments.

## Component upgrades vs full rebuilds

Sometimes you keep a moulding dream but cheap out on glazing temporarily—document **planned Phase 2** so you do not paint yourself into conservation corners you cannot retrofit without surgery.

## Appraisals and riders

Fine art insurers sometimes ask for **condition photos pre-framing**. If you are already spending, capture raking light shots of the naked sheet—future claims appreciate receipts that predate glazing.

## DIY partially-done stacks

Customers occasionally supply **their own mats** cut elsewhere to save labor bucks—confirm **bevel quality** and **thickness caliper truth** before assuming a discount. Mis-cut windows become surprise rush charges anyway.

## Price anchoring with comps

If a local shop quote shocks you, ask for **SKU-level** comparables—identical moulding meter, identical UV class—before declaring defeat. Apples-to-rot comparisons exhaust everyone.
